Industry & Product News

Cochlear's Nucleus Nexa: The World's First Upgradeable "Smart" Implant System

On July 8, 2025, Cochlear Limited announced FDA approval of the Cochlear™ Nucleus® Nexa™ System — the world's first and only smart cochlear implant system. The breakthrough lies in its architecture: until now, recipients have been limited to accessing new technology through the external sound processor; the new Nexa System features upgradeable implant firmware, enabling recipients to access future innovation through both their implant and sound processor for the first time. The portfolio includes the Nucleus Nexa Implant, the Kanso® 3 Nexa Sound Processor, and the Nucleus 8 Nexa Sound Processor — and those with legacy Nucleus Systems will be able to upgrade to the new Kanso 3 Sound Processor.

Starkey & MED-EL Launch DualSync Bimodal Streaming Partnership

Starkey and MED-EL have announced DualSync — a bimodal streaming partnership enabling Apple technology to seamlessly stream to compatible Starkey hearing aids and MED-EL cochlear implants simultaneously. Beginning July 1, 2025, Starkey Edge AI and Genesis AI hearing aids and MED-EL SONNET 3, SONNET 2, SONNET, and RONDO 3 audio processors became compatible for bimodal streaming. For patients relying on both devices, this is a meaningful quality-of-life win — a unified wireless audio experience long-awaited by the bimodal community.

New CPT Hearing Aid Service Codes Stir Professional Debate

On July 15, 2025, the Academy of Doctors of Audiology (ADA) notified members about newly announced CPT hearing aid service codes, shepherded by the American Academy of Audiology and the American Speech-Language-Hearing Association, and voiced concerns about the codes and the timeline for their implementation. The announcement has sparked discussion across professional associations about reimbursement equity and the pace of coding changes. Audiologists are encouraged to review ADA communications and engage with their professional organizations ahead of implementation.

Gene Therapy Reverses Congenital Deafness in Children and Adults — Within Weeks

Published July 20, a multicenter clinical trial found that using gene therapy to treat hereditary deafness is safe and effective in both children and adults. Researchers used a virus to deliver a healthy copy of the OTOF gene into the inner ear, improving auditory function across all ten participants, with results working best in young children — one 7-year-old girl regained almost full hearing — but still benefiting adults. Published in Nature Medicine, the findings show much of the hearing restoration occurred within one month of drug delivery, improving outcomes from complete deafness to moderate hearing loss in patients with OTOF gene mutations.

Source: Karolinska Institutet / UC Irvine School of Medicine | Nature Medicine, July 2025

Lenire Bimodal Neuromodulation: 91.5% of Patients Report Significant Tinnitus Improvement

A 2025 study of 212 people who sought help for moderate to severe tinnitus at an audiology clinic found that 91.5 percent reported a significant improvement after using Lenire. At the start of the study, about half had severe to "catastrophic" tinnitus — that rate dropped to just 11 percent after treatment, while those with moderate tinnitus fell from roughly half to 22 percent. Lenire works by combining gentle electrical pulses to the tongue with carefully calibrated sound tones delivered through Bluetooth headphones, retraining the brain's response to tinnitus.

Source: AARP Health / Byrom Audiology, July 2025

Machine Learning for Cochlear Implant Outcomes: Systematic Review Published in npj Digital Medicine

A systematic review published July 5 in npj Digital Medicine critically examines the current literature on the application of machine learning techniques for predicting cochlear implant outcomes. The review arrives as AI-driven programming tools become an active area of clinical development, and provides a much-needed evidence baseline for what ML can — and cannot — currently deliver. Machine learning integration promises devices that automatically adapt to individual preferences and acoustic environments, continuously optimizing performance without manual adjustments — though the review underscores that robust, prospective validation remains the next frontier.

Source: npj Digital Medicine, Vol. 8, July 5, 2025

APD Systematic Review: Rehab Dominates, But Diagnostic Standardization Still Lacking

Auditory Processing Disorder manifests as impaired auditory information processing despite normal peripheral hearing, and current clinical protocols lack standardization, hampering evidence-based intervention development. A major systematic review analyzing APD research from 2011–2025 (156 studies, PRISMA methodology) found that medical interventions were markedly underrepresented compared to rehabilitative approaches, with the Random Gap Detection Test and Dichotic Digits Difference Test most frequently used diagnostically, while cognitive skill enhancement dominated intervention strategies (52.8%), followed by computer-based rehabilitation (26.4%).
