July 24, 2025

This week brought a landmark FDA clearance in cochlear implant technology, a striking gene therapy result out of Karolinska Institutet, and a major European study with urgent implications for how we counsel patients about hearing loss, loneliness, and cognitive decline. Here's everything you need to know heading into the weekend.

Industry & Product News

Cochlear Launches World's First Upgradeable "Smart" Implant System: Nucleus Nexa

Cochlear Limited announced FDA approval of the Cochlear™ Nucleus® Nexa™ System — described as the world's first and only smart cochlear implant system. The headline innovation: until now, recipients have been limited to accessing new technology through the external sound processor. The new Nexa System features upgradeable implant firmware, enabling recipients to access future innovations through both the implant and the sound processor. The portfolio includes the Nucleus Nexa Implant, the Nucleus Kanso® 3 Nexa Sound Processor, and the Nucleus 8 Nexa Sound Processor — with legacy Nucleus System users also able to upgrade to the new Kanso 3.

Starkey Omega AI: Fifth-Gen AI Hearing Aid Earns Top Lab Scores

Starkey's Omega AI represents the company's fifth generation of AI-powered hearing aids, replacing the Edge AI line and marking a major leap in both sound processing and user-focused design. At the core is the new G3 Gen AI Neuro Processor, which powers DNN 360 — a deep-neural-network-based directionality system that analyzes the entire sound environment to deliver more natural, 360-degree hearing, rather than focusing only on sounds in front of the wearer. A generative AI–powered TeleHear AI feature can identify and help resolve common hearing aid issues with up to 93% predictive accuracy, and updated ProFit 2025.1 fitting software introduces Session Notes, Acoustic Model Optimization 2.0, and a REM Target Match simulator for clinicians.

→ Read the Starkey press release

New CPT Hearing Aid Service Codes Spark Industry Debate

On July 15, 2025, the Academy of Doctors of Audiology (ADA) notified members about newly announced CPT hearing aid service codes shepherded by the American Academy of Audiology and the American Speech-Language-Hearing Association, and voiced concerns about the codes and the process and timeline for their implementation. The development reflects ongoing tensions in audiology's billing landscape as the profession navigates scope-of-practice and reimbursement reform. Clinicians are encouraged to monitor their professional associations for guidance on how the new codes may affect existing workflows and revenue cycles.

MarkeTrak 2025: OTC Devices and Earbuds Reshape the Consumer Landscape

The twelfth edition of the MarkeTrak consumer survey expands in response to a shifting market, integrating new data on prescription hearing aids, over-the-counter devices, and emerging technologies such as earbuds with hearing improvement features. The report, published this week by the Hearing Industries Association, is a key resource for tracking consumer adoption trends and attitudes — with implications for how clinicians position their services in an increasingly crowded device marketplace. Research & Clinical

Single-Injection Gene Therapy Restores Hearing in Children and Adults Within Weeks

🔬 Landmark Study

Using gene therapy to treat hereditary deafness is safe and effective in both children and adults, according to new findings from a multicenter clinical trial published July 20, 2025. Researchers used a virus to deliver a healthy copy of the OTOF gene into the inner ear, improving auditory function across all ten participants; the therapy worked best in young children but still benefited adults, with one 7-year-old girl regaining almost full hearing. Published in Nature Medicine, the findings show gene therapy's potential for fast results, with much of the hearing restoration occurring within one month of drug delivery — improving patients from complete deafness to moderate hearing loss.

→ Read the UC Irvine/Karolinska release

Hearing Loss + Loneliness = Accelerated Memory Decline: Major European Study

Research from the University of Geneva analyzing 33,741 older adults across Europe found that hearing loss accelerates cognitive decline, particularly when combined with feelings of loneliness — with the study tracking participants for up to 18 years. A particularly striking finding: those who feel lonely despite being socially connected — termed "lonely-in-the-crowd" — face the greatest cognitive vulnerability when hearing loss is present. The authors note that early hearing interventions, like hearing aids, can be effective preventive tools, particularly for those who are socially engaged but hindered by hearing difficulties.

Lenire Bimodal Neuromodulation Shows Strong Real-World Tinnitus Results

In a 2025 study of 212 people who sought help for moderate to severe tinnitus at an audiology clinic in Alaska, 91.5 percent reported significant improvement after using Lenire. At the start, about half had severe to "catastrophic" tinnitus — a figure that dropped to 11 percent after treatment; the other half with moderate tinnitus fell from 50% to 22%. Lenire uses gentle electrical pulses to the tongue combined with calibrated sound tones delivered through Bluetooth headphones to retrain the brain's response to tinnitus. The results add to a growing body of real-world evidence supporting bimodal neuromodulation as a frontline clinical tool.

Machine Learning for Cochlear Implant Outcomes: New Systematic Review in npj Digital Medicine

Published July 5, 2025 in npj Digital Medicine, a new systematic review critically examines the current literature on machine learning techniques for predicting cochlear implant outcomes — noting that CIs have transformed the lives of over one million individuals with hearing impairment, including children as young as nine months. The review provides a timely synthesis for clinicians and researchers as AI-assisted fitting and outcome prediction tools enter the clinical mainstream, potentially reducing the time and guesswork involved in post-implant rehabilitation planning.

→ Read the paper in npj Digital Medicine

APD Diagnostic Landscape Still Lacks Standardization, Comprehensive Review Finds

Auditory Processing Disorder manifests as impaired auditory information processing despite normal peripheral hearing — yet current clinical protocols still lack standardization, hampering evidence-based intervention development. A major PRISMA-guided review published in the Journal of Clinical Medicine, analyzing 156 studies, found that medical interventions were markedly underrepresented compared to rehabilitative approaches, with the Random Gap Detection Test and Dichotic Digits Difference Test used most frequently; cognitive skill enhancement dominated intervention strategies (52.8%), followed by computer-based rehabilitation (26.4%). The authors conclude that a hierarchical processing framework is recommended for assessment and treatment, with future research prioritizing large-scale controlled trials and unified diagnostic criteria.

Clinical & Community

Hearing Workforce Growing, But Rural Shortages Remain Acute

The hearing health workforce — including audiologists and hearing instrument specialists — grew by more than 83% between 2012 and 2022. However, researchers identified that 75% of U.S. counties still meet the criteria for shortage areas, where the number of professionals is insufficient to meet estimated local needs. The finding underscores persistent geographic inequity in hearing care access and puts pressure on workforce pipeline programs — programs that may themselves face headwinds: funding cuts at the CDC and HRSA threaten Early Hearing Detection and Intervention (EHDI)-related programs, with data collection, follow-up, and staff capacity potentially at risk.

Cleveland Clinic Study Supports CI Candidacy Beyond FDA-Labeled Criteria

At the 2025 American Cochlear Implant Alliance (ACIA) annual meeting, the Cleveland Clinic Hearing Implant Program presented compelling research supporting the safety and efficacy of cochlear implantation in patients who exceeded current FDA-labeled candidacy criteria. The study is part of an ongoing effort to increase access to cochlear implants for patients with significant hearing needs. This work aligns with a growing movement to expand access: current research validates cochlear implantation for individuals with moderate to severe hearing loss who experience poor speech clarity despite optimal hearing aid fitting.

→ Read the Cleveland Clinic Q/D post

Hearing Aids & Implants Shown to Improve Social Lives — New JAMA Otolaryngology Study

A new study from the USC Caruso Department of Otolaryngology, published in JAMA Otolaryngology – Head & Neck Surgery, is the first to link hearing aids and cochlear implants to improved social lives among adults with hearing loss. The finding provides clinicians with a powerful new talking point for patient counseling: treatment is not just about decibels, but about quality of connection. Combined with this week's loneliness-cognition data from Geneva, the case for early, proactive hearing intervention — and patient follow-up — has never been stronger.
